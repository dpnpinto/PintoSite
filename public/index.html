Meet Zezoca: My Personal AI (Running on Arch Linux!)

Posted on July 13, 2026 at 00:00 +00

Having an AI is fantastic. But having a personal AI running in a purely minimalist environment, controlled directly by you and configured entirely by hand? That is on a whole other level.

Allow me to introduce you to Zezoca, my custom personal AI.

I've always believed that true engineering requires absolute control over your tools. That's why Zezoca lives on the most beloved distribution in the Linux universe: Arch Linux. I didn't want a bloated, locked-down system. I wanted a lean, keyboard-driven environment where every single package is there for a reason. Interacting with Zezoca directly from the terminal, tweaking configurations in Vim, and managing the workflow inside my DWM setup makes the whole experience incredibly lightweight and efficient.

This isn't just about calling an external API; it's about getting under the hood. Running my own AI locally gives me the freedom to dive into the architecture, optimize parallel matrix calculations using raw C and CUDA, and truly understand how the underlying models operate. It perfectly bridges my passion for hardcore system administration and artificial intelligence.

In my latest video, I roll up my sleeves and walk you through how I built and deployed Zezoca on my Arch Linux System. If you want to see what happens when the minimalist Linux philosophy meets AI, check out the video or github below!

Projects using a Pi

Posted on June 4, 2026 at 00:00 +00

Welcome to the magical, sometimes maddening world of tiny computers and endless possibilities! If you've ever looked at a Raspberry Pi—perhaps a tiny Zero 2 W—and thought, "I bet I can make this do something incredibly cool before I accidentally short-circuit something," you are exactly in the right place.

Below, you'll find a collection of open-source projects I've cooked up. Think of them as your survival guide for dipping your toes into the wild waters of systems engineering. Whether you are trying to understand how things actually work under the hood, or you just want to experience the monumental triumph of making a piece of silicon do what you tell it to, these projects are the perfect starting point for beginners.

The best part? No expensive mainframes were harmed in the making of these tutorials. Everything is built on the philosophy of low-cost hardware and the sheer power of open-source software.
